2. Executive Summary

A mobile tourist application that uses AI-powered photo recognition to identify attractions and provide descriptions via text and audio. The platform connects tourists with signed partner businesses for monetization through commissions, advertising, and loyalty programs.

This document presents 3 architecture options ranging from a lean MVP ($240k) to a full-featured platform ($1.65M), with a recommendation for Option 2: Progressive Build as the best balance of cost, risk, and feature delivery.

3. Problem Statement

3.1. Current User Pain Point

Tourists currently follow this flow to learn about attractions:

  1. Take a photo of an attraction

  2. Open ChatGPT or Wikipedia

  3. Manually search for information

  4. Read/listen separately

  5. Search again for nearby restaurants, cafes, activities

This is fragmented, slow, and has no monetization path.

3.2. Proposed Solution

A single mobile app that replaces the entire flow:

  1. User photographs an attraction

  2. AI identifies the attraction (95% target accuracy)

  3. App delivers a text + audio description

  4. App recommends signed partner businesses nearby

  5. User receives QR-code discounts at partners

  6. Loyalty program incentivizes repeat usage

3.3. Business Model

  • Partner commissions: 10-20% on transactions facilitated through the app

  • Partner advertising: Promoted placement in recommendation results

  • Scale ambition: Klook/Viator level (50M+ users) over 3-5 years

6.8.2. Custom ML Model — How It Works

At 250k+ MAU, 80% of photos are the same ~1,000 well-known landmarks. Instead of calling Google Vision + OpenAI every time, a fine-tuned image classifier (EfficientNet on SageMaker) recognizes known landmarks in ~50-100ms at ~$0.0002/call — vs $0.012-0.032/call externally. Pre-curated descriptions are served from PostgreSQL. Unknown landmarks (20%) still fall back to the Vision → GPT-4V pipeline.

6.10. China Market Entry — Architecture Reference

When the business decides to enter the Chinese market, the same code and Terraform modules deploy to the China region. This is a ~6-8 week product initiative (Chinese content, partners, WeChat Pay/Alipay, ICP license, PIPL compliance) — infrastructure deploy itself takes hours, but the surrounding product work is the real effort. The diagram below shows the full multi-region state after deployment.

The app is technically usable from China without dedicated infrastructure — Chinese tourists visiting the US use the US-hosted app with no issues. However, serving users physically in China with Chinese landmarks requires the China region because: (1) GFW throttles international traffic (200-400ms added latency), (2) Google Play is blocked (need Chinese Android stores), (3) Stripe doesn’t support WeChat Pay/Alipay, (4) PIPL requires Chinese user data to stay in mainland China when deliberately targeting Chinese consumers.
China’s PIPL (Personal Information Protection Law), CSL (Cybersecurity Law), and DSL (Data Security Law) require that personal data of Chinese users stays within mainland China. This means each region runs its own primary database for local users — a simple read replica is not legally compliant. Furthermore, landmarks are geographically specific (Great Wall vs. Statue of Liberty), so there is no shared catalog to sync. The two regions are fully independent — zero cross-region data transfer.
Figure 5. Option 2 Architecture — With China Region Deployed

China region key differences:

  • Route 53 latency-based routing — directs users to nearest region automatically

  • Fully independent databases — each region has its own primary PostgreSQL with its own users, partners, and landmark catalog. Landmarks are geographically specific (Chinese landmarks in CN, US landmarks in US), so there is nothing to sync. Zero cross-region data transfer — fully PIPL/CSL/DSL compliant

  • Fully independent S3 buckets — user photos and landmark assets stay in the region where they were created. No cross-region replication needed

  • Gemini-only AI — OpenAI is blocked in China; Lambda uses Google Gemini exclusively

  • ICP license required — serving content in China requires an ICP filing (2-4 weeks lead time); must be initiated early in the China market entry initiative

  • Same container image — identical ECS monolith with region-specific environment config (API keys, DB endpoints, AI provider)

  • Shared Cognito — single identity provider; user accounts authenticate via Cognito regardless of region. A Chinese tourist visiting the US is routed to US region and sees US landmarks — correct by design

  • Estimated additional OPEX: ~$400-500/mo (mirrors US compute + data, no separate AI costs since Gemini is shared)

This section describes the migration path at 500k+ MAU — when the monolith genuinely reaches its limits. Not relevant until then (see Scalability & Growth Roadmap for the 1k–250k path).

At this scale, the architecture evolves toward:

  • EKS + Istio service mesh — decompose modules into independent services for 3+ teams

  • Aurora Global DB — cross-region replication, auto-scaling storage

  • Kafka for high-throughput event streaming (replacing SQS for ordering/replay guarantees)

  • Multi-region active-active deployment (US, China, EU)

8.1. Trigger Conditions

  • MAU exceeds 500k+ and growing

  • Team has grown to 15+ and needs microservices team boundaries

  • Monthly revenue justifies $50k+/mo infrastructure spend

  • Business requires multi-region active-active presence (not just prepared — genuinely active)

9. Technical Deep Dives

9.1. AI Recognition Strategy

The AI pipeline uses a multi-provider fallback chain to achieve 95% accuracy:

User uploads photo
    ↓
Google Vision API (Fast landmark detection, ~200ms)
    ↓ Found landmark?
    ├── Yes → OpenAI GPT-4V (Rich description generation)
    │         ↓ Failed?
    │         └── Google Gemini (Fallback LLM)
    ├── No landmark → Gemini general description
    └── Offline → On-device ML model (70-80% accuracy)
    ↓
Result cached in Redis (24h TTL) → S3 (permanent archive)

Why multi-provider:

  • No single AI provider is 100% reliable or available in all regions

  • Google Vision excels at landmark detection (fast, cheap)

  • GPT-4V generates the richest natural-language descriptions

  • Gemini serves as fallback and primary provider in China (where OpenAI may be blocked)

  • On-device ML enables offline mode and reduces cloud API costs

Cost optimization:

  • Redis caching avoids re-processing the same attraction photos

  • SQS decouples the upload from recognition (async processing)

  • Lambda pricing = pay only for actual recognition invocations

11. Assumptions & Constraints

11.1. Assumptions

  1. Budget was not discussed — we assume a Year 1 investment capacity of $500k–$600k (CAPEX + team + OPEX). Option 2 fits within this range at ~$792k TCO including team costs. If the actual budget is materially different, prioritization may change.

  2. The application will be popular — therefore we need an architecture that can scale. The progressive approach (Option 2) validates before investing in scale infrastructure.

  3. Offline mode is essential for tourists — areas like mountain vineyards may have poor connectivity. On-device ML is deferred to the Full phase to reduce initial complexity. Basic offline caching of previously viewed content is included in MVP.

  4. US is the priority market — we deploy to US only. Chinese tourists visiting the US use the US-hosted app with no issues. The architecture is region-portable (same code and Terraform modules), but entering China is a ~6-8 week product initiative (Chinese content, partners, WeChat Pay/Alipay, ICP license, PIPL compliance) — not just an infrastructure deploy.

  5. Partner onboarding is manual initially — the client team signs partners and enters their information. A self-service partner portal comes in Phase 2 (Full).

  6. No real-time AR overlays — the flow is: take photo → get description. Not: point camera → see live overlays. This significantly reduces mobile complexity.

  7. Team of 5 developers — stable throughout the project. No mid-project scaling. See [Team Structure] for role breakdown.

  8. Risk buffer of 20-50% — applied per phase based on complexity. AI integration and dual-platform native mobile carry the highest uncertainty.

11.2. Constraints

  • China data residency: When China is deployed, personal data of Chinese users must remain in mainland China (PIPL/CSL/DSL). Architecture is region-portable — same code and Terraform modules.

  • PCI-DSS compliance: Payment card data must be tokenized through Stripe — never stored in our systems

  • GDPR readiness: Data collection, consent, and right-to-erasure mechanisms designed from Day 1

  • AI API availability: OpenAI may be blocked or restricted in China — Gemini serves as primary provider there

  • App Store approval: Both Apple App Store and Google Play require review cycles (1-2 weeks each). Timeline includes a 2-week review block.
